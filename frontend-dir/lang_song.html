Introduction to Lang Song Minor Seminary

Address: Quang Van Hamlet, Phuoc Thuan Commune, Tuy Phuoc District, Binh Dinh Province.

Opening Hours (approximate): 7:00 AM – 11:30 AM and 2:00 PM – 5:30 PM daily (Closed on retreat days – announcements will be made accordingly).

Lang Song Minor Seminary (also known as Lang Song Church) is located about 10km northeast of Quy Nhon city center. The name "Lang Song" translates to "River Village," referring to its picturesque setting amidst waterways and lush green rice fields. Between 1841 and 1850, the seminary was originally constructed with modest materials—bamboo walls and thatched roofs. It wasn’t until 1927 that a larger, more solid church was built on a 2,000 m² plot of land. Later, the printing house of the Diocese of Dong Dang Trong and the bishop’s residence were also established on the site.

Under the direction of Father Paul Maheu—who had studied printing in Hong Kong—the Lang Song printing house became one of the three most significant centers in Vietnam for literature in the Romanized Vietnamese script and for promoting its use.

To reach Lang Song Minor Seminary from Quy Nhon, visitors can follow National Highway 19. Along the way, they can also explore other notable attractions in Binh Dinh, such as Long Phuoc Martial Arts Pagoda, Thien Hung Pagoda, and Banh It Towers.

The Significance of Lang Song Church

In the past, Lang Song Church primarily served as a monastery for minor seminarians to study theology and cultivate their spiritual path. After completing their training, these seminarians would move on to major seminaries to further their journey toward priesthood. At its peak, nearly 200 seminarians from across the Central Coastal provinces of Vietnam studied and lived on the premises.

Today, the church no longer functions as a seminary but has become a spiritual destination and cultural tourism site. On important occasions—such as Catholic holidays or the Lunar New Year—both locals and tourists visit to attend mass, offer prayers, enjoy the peaceful atmosphere, and explore the historical printing exhibition housed within the church grounds.

What Makes Lang Song Minor Seminary in Quy Nhơn So Special?

3.1 Discovering the Unique Gothic Architecture

With a history spanning over 170 years, Lang Song Minor Seminary still preserves its distinctive Gothic architecture. Upon entering the church grounds, visitors are drawn to the central chapel—featuring three main entrances and sixteen exquisitely carved doors supported by massive wooden columns. On either side are the former working and study areas of seminarians. The sanctuary’s intricately carved altar, religious statues, and detailed reliefs highlight the hallmark elements of Gothic design, including arched doorways and pointed vaults.

3.2 Check-in at Lang Song Minor Seminary

Thanks to its ancient charm and unique aesthetic, Lang Song Minor Seminary is a perfect check-in spot for travelers who love classic architecture. Iconic features include the towering main arch gate with intricate decorative patterns, long hallways with gently curved arches that create an ideal backdrop for memorable photos, and the impressive statue of the Virgin Mary framing the entire facade of the church.

3.3 Visit the Lang Song Printing House Exhibition Room

The exhibition room of the Lang Song Printing House is a fascinating destination for those interested in the history of printing in Vietnam. Step inside to experience a vintage atmosphere filled with hundreds of carefully preserved documents and ancient books—such as Tục Ngữ An Nam, Tập đọc, and Tập đánh vần ABC. The documents are arranged in chronological order, offering a clear insight into the evolution of printing over the decades.

Originally built in 1872 and rebuilt in 1904 under Father Paul Maheu’s guidance, the printing house played a pivotal role until it was relocated in 1936.

Important Notes When Visiting Lang Song Seminary

  • Do not litter: Always keep the environment clean and dispose of trash properly.
  • Keep your voice down: Maintain a quiet, respectful atmosphere—avoid loud conversations or noises.
  • Respect ongoing religious practices: If a ceremony or mass is taking place, observe respectfully and avoid interruption.
  • Do not touch or deface structures: Refrain from touching religious statues or drawing on walls and architectural features.
  • Sun protection: Bring a hat, cap, or sunscreen if visiting during summer.
  • Dress appropriately: Wear comfortable yet modest clothing, especially when entering the church.
  • Follow rules and guidance: Adhere to the regulations set by the church management and do not enter restricted areas without permission.
